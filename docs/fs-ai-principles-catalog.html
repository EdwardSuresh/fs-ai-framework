How these principles were derived

Each principle is derived from an obligation or failure mode specific to regulated financial services: model risk management expectations, suitability and best-interest standards, fair underwriting and anti-discrimination law, books-and-records and supervision duties, privacy and MNPI controls, algorithmic trading safeguards, and operational resilience requirements. The generating logic is the sector itself — not a generic productivity framework.

Template & usage

Each principle follows the TOGAF Architecture Principles template — Name, Statement, Rationale, Implications — extended with a sector example and indicative regulatory anchors. The catalog is intended as a governed EA repository artifact used in ARB intake, design review, and go-live gating for any AI initiative.

Trust & Accountability

FS-AI-01 — FS-AI-04

Who answers for the system, who is allowed to advise, what must be explainable, and whose interest the system serves. These principles exist because in financial services accountability is personal, advice is licensed, and reasons are legally required.

FS-AI-01

Accountable Human Ownership

CM · WM · INS
Statement
Every AI system shall have a named, accountable business owner and a designated model owner. Accountability for decisions and outcomes remains with identified humans and is never delegated to the system.
Rationale
Regulators hold firms and individuals accountable — never algorithms. Without named ownership there is no one to accept risk, fund remediation, or answer a regulator, and "the model did it" is not a defense.
Implications
  • The AI inventory records a business owner and model owner for every system; orphaned systems are restricted or decommissioned.
  • Risk acceptance for AI use is signed by the accountable owner, not by the delivery team.
  • Ownership transfer is mandatory on role change, verified in periodic attestation.
Example (WM): A robo-rebalancing model has a named first-line owner and a second-line model risk officer; both appear in the model inventory entry.
Anchors
Model risk governance expectations (e.g., SR 11-7 / OCC 2011-12 style); senior accountability regimes; NAIC AI model governance guidance.
FS-AI-02

Licensed Advice Boundary

WM · INS · (CM retail)
Statement
No AI-generated output shall constitute investment, insurance, or financial advice to a client unless it is reviewed and adopted by an appropriately licensed professional who assumes responsibility for it.
Rationale
Suitability, best-interest, and producer-licensing obligations attach to licensed individuals and supervised firms. AI cannot hold a license, owe a fiduciary duty, or be sanctioned — so it cannot be the source of advice.
Implications
  • Client-facing AI is positioned and labeled as information or assistance, never advice; disclosures are standardized.
  • Advice-adjacent outputs flow through a licensed-human approval workflow before reaching clients.
  • Supervisory procedures explicitly cover AI-assisted communications and recommendations.
Example (WM): GenAI drafts portfolio commentary; the advisor reviews, edits, and sends it under their own name and supervision chain.
Anchors
Best-interest / suitability standards (e.g., Reg BI-style); investment adviser fiduciary duty; state insurance producer licensing; supervision rules.
FS-AI-03

Explainability Proportional to Impact

CM · WM · INS
Statement
AI-influenced decisions affecting client outcomes — underwriting, pricing, suitability, claims, credit — shall be explainable in plain language to the affected client and in technical detail to regulators and auditors, with rigor scaled to decision impact.
Rationale
Adverse-action and claim-decision obligations require stated reasons. Where the law demands a reason, an unexplainable model is an unusable model — regardless of its accuracy.
Implications
  • Model selection for high-impact decisions weighs explainability as a first-class criterion, not an afterthought.
  • Reason codes / key-factor outputs are produced for every adverse client decision.
  • Opaque models in high-impact uses require compensating controls: surrogate explanations plus mandatory human review, or they are not used.
Example (INS): An auto-underwriting model emits the top contributing factors for every declined application, feeding the adverse-action notice.
Anchors
Adverse-action requirements (ECOA-style); fair claims handling standards; insurance AI circulars (e.g., NY DFS-style); supervisory expectations.
FS-AI-04

Client Best Interest by Design

WM · INS · (CM retail)
Statement
AI systems that recommend, rank, or price products shall embed best-interest, suitability, and fair-treatment obligations as design constraints in the optimization objective — not as post-hoc compliance checks.
Rationale
An AI optimizer pursues exactly what it is told to maximize. If revenue or denial-rate is the objective and client interest is only a filter, conflicts of interest are built into the mathematics of the system.
Implications
  • Optimization objectives and reward functions are documented and reviewed for conflicts at design time.
  • Recommendation engines are constrained to the suitable product universe for the specific client, regardless of margin.
  • Claims and service AI shall not be optimized on denial rates, delay, or attrition of valid claims.
Example (WM): The recommendation engine excludes any product outside the client's documented risk profile — even when it carries the highest margin.
Anchors
Best-interest standards; conflict-of-interest disclosure obligations; unfair trade practice / unfair claims practice statutes.

Risk & Compliance

FS-AI-05 — FS-AI-08

How AI is inventoried, validated, traced, tested for bias, and gated before it touches production. These principles translate model risk management and supervision duties into architecture requirements.

FS-AI-05

Model Risk Management for All AI

CM · WM · INS
Statement
Every AI model — including generative AI, agentic workflows, and vendor-embedded AI — shall be captured in the model inventory, risk-tiered by materiality, and independently validated in proportion to its tier, before use and periodically thereafter.
Rationale
Model risk arises from incorrect outputs and from correct outputs used incorrectly. GenAI, prompts, agents, and third-party AI are models in substance; excluding them from MRM creates an ungoverned shadow estate.
Implications
  • The inventory definition of "model" explicitly includes LLM applications, prompts/agents with material effect, and vendor AI features.
  • A documented tiering method (client impact, financial exposure, regulatory sensitivity, autonomy) drives validation depth.
  • An independent validation function exists with authority to restrict or reject; unvalidated high-tier models do not reach production.
Example (CM): A pre-trade analytics LLM assistant is tiered high because it influences trading decisions, and is validated before desk rollout.
Anchors
Supervisory model risk guidance (SR 11-7 / OCC style); insurance model governance bulletins; third-party risk management expectations.
FS-AI-06

Regulatory Traceability of Every Decision

CM · WM · INS
Statement
Every AI-influenced decision shall be reconstructable end-to-end — input data and versions, model and prompt versions, output, and subsequent human action — and retained in accordance with books-and-records obligations.
Rationale
Regulators and courts ask "why did the system do that, on that date, for that client?" Years later. Without decision lineage there is no defensible answer, and AI-generated business records carry the same retention duties as any other record.
Implications
  • A decision-logging standard defines the minimum captured for every AI-influenced decision.
  • Audit trails are tamper-evident; retention schedules align to records rules (including immutable storage where required).
  • Reconstruction capability is tested — periodically replaying a past decision is an audit procedure, not a hope.
Example (WM): A 2024 rebalancing recommendation is reconstructed in a 2026 exam: model v3.2, the market-data snapshot used, and the advisor's approval.
Anchors
Books-and-records rules (SEC 17a-4 / FINRA 4511 style); supervision and audit-trail expectations; claims-file documentation standards.
FS-AI-07

Fairness Testing Before & After Deployment

WM · INS · credit-adjacent CM
Statement
AI used in underwriting, pricing, credit, claims, or eligibility decisions shall be tested for unlawful bias and disparate impact — including proxy discrimination through correlated variables and third-party data — before deployment, and monitored continuously in production.
Rationale
AI can discriminate without ever seeing a protected attribute, through proxies like geography, purchase history, or external scores. Fair-lending and insurance anti-discrimination law applies to outcomes, not intentions.
Implications
  • Pre-deployment disparate-impact analysis is a mandatory validation step for in-scope models, with documented thresholds.
  • Proxy-variable review covers all inputs, including vendor data and external scores.
  • Production fairness monitoring exists with a defined remediation path when thresholds are breached.
Example (INS): A life-underwriting model using external lifestyle data is tested to confirm those scores do not function as a proxy for protected class.
Anchors
Anti-discrimination law in credit and insurance (ECOA / FHA style); state insurance AI fairness bulletins; regulator guidance on external data and proxies.
FS-AI-08

Compliance Gates Precede Capability

CM · WM · INS
Statement
No AI capability shall reach production — and no material change shall be promoted — without passing defined compliance, legal, and risk gates. Surveillance and supervision shall extend to AI-generated content and AI-initiated actions.
Rationale
GenAI experimentation moves faster than control frameworks. Supervision duties over communications, recommendations, and trading do not lapse because a machine produced the content — ungated capability is unsupervised activity.
Implications
  • A stage-gate path (intake → tiering → validation → compliance/legal review → pilot → production) is mandatory for all AI initiatives.
  • E-communications surveillance and trade surveillance ingest AI-assisted outputs and actions.
  • Experimentation sandboxes are segregated from production data and client channels.
Example (CM): A research-summarization tool passes compliance review of disclaimers and information-barrier controls before analysts may use it.
Anchors
Supervisory system requirements (FINRA 3110 style); communications-with-the-public rules; change governance expectations in MRM guidance.

Data & Model Integrity

FS-AI-09 — FS-AI-11

What data AI may touch, what knowledge it may speak from, and how models and prompts change. These principles protect confidentiality, truthfulness, and reproducibility.

FS-AI-09

Data Sovereignty & Confidentiality Boundaries

CM · WM · INS
Statement
Client PII, account and policy data, MNPI, and proprietary firm data shall be processed only within approved, governed AI boundaries. No such data shall be submitted to unapproved external AI services, and vendor terms shall prohibit training on firm data.
Rationale
Privacy law, confidentiality duties, and insider-information controls all break the moment client or material non-public data enters an ungoverned model. Shadow AI usage is the single largest leakage vector in the GenAI era.
Implications
  • An approved AI service catalog defines where each data classification may be processed, including residency constraints.
  • DLP and network controls block client/MNPI data from unapproved AI endpoints.
  • Vendor contracts include no-training clauses, data segregation, and deletion rights; these are verified, not assumed.
Example (WM): Advisors use the firm's governed assistant for client work; public chatbots are blocked for client data via DLP policy.
Anchors
Financial privacy rules (GLBA / Reg S-P style); MNPI and information-barrier controls; cross-border data residency requirements.
FS-AI-10

Grounded, Approved Knowledge Only

CM · WM · INS
Statement
Client-facing and decision-supporting AI outputs shall be grounded in approved, current, and traceable knowledge sources, with citations where practicable. Ungrounded generation shall never be presented as fact.
Rationale
A hallucinated performance figure, policy term, or tax rule is a misrepresentation with liability attached. Communications standards require content that is fair, balanced, and not misleading — a bar free generation cannot reliably meet.
Implications
  • Retrieval-augmented patterns against governed repositories are the default for factual outputs.
  • Knowledge bases have owners and freshness SLAs; stale sources are flagged or excluded.
  • Hallucination and factual-accuracy testing is part of validation for client-facing systems.
Example (INS): The policyholder chatbot answers only from filed policy documents and cites the specific clause it relied on.
Anchors
Communications-with-the-public standards; misrepresentation and unfair-practice statutes; disclosure accuracy obligations.
FS-AI-11

Model & Prompt Change Control

CM · WM · INS
Statement
Models, prompts, agents, and their configurations shall be versioned and promoted only through controlled change management. No silent change shall reach production — including vendor-initiated foundation-model updates.
Rationale
A single upstream LLM version change can alter the behavior of every dependent workflow overnight. Validation, traceability (FS-AI-06), and reproducibility are meaningless without version stability.
Implications
  • Prompts and configurations live under version control with review and approval, like code.
  • Vendor agreements require notice of model updates; regression test suites run before adoption.
  • Champion–challenger evaluation and documented rollback paths exist for material changes.
Example (CM): A vendor LLM upgrade is regression-tested against a benchmark suite before any trading-desk assistant switches versions.
Anchors
Change-management expectations in model risk guidance; IT general controls; third-party risk management standards.

Resilience & Market Integrity

FS-AI-12 — FS-AI-14

How AI behaves when things go wrong, what hard limits bound its autonomy in markets, and how degradation is detected before it becomes an incident.

FS-AI-12

Fail to Safe, Fail to Human

CM · WM · INS
Statement
AI system failure or degraded confidence shall result in a designed safe fallback — human handling, an established manual process, or a conservative default. Silent failure and unbounded autonomous behavior under degradation are prohibited.
Rationale
Outages and degradations are certainties, not possibilities. In regulated workflows a silent failure becomes a conduct or resilience incident: unpaid valid claims, mishandled orders, unsupervised advice.
Implications
  • Every AI-dependent workflow documents its degraded mode and the trigger conditions for it.
  • Confidence thresholds route low-certainty cases to humans by design.
  • Resilience and incident-response testing explicitly includes AI dependencies and their fallbacks.
Example (INS): When claims-triage AI is unavailable, all claims route to the standard manual queue with the regulatory SLA clock intact.
Anchors
Operational resilience regimes (DORA-style); business continuity expectations; fair claims handling timeliness standards.
FS-AI-13

Market Integrity & Autonomy Limits

CM
Statement
AI participating in trading, order handling, or other market-facing activity shall operate within hard autonomy limits — pre-trade risk controls, throttles, and tested kill switches — enforced in a control layer outside the model's reach, and shall be conformance-tested before deployment.
Rationale
Algorithmic trading rules require limits, testing, and immediate shutdown capability. Learning systems optimizing freely can discover manipulative or destabilizing strategies; the safeguard must be architectural, not behavioral.
Implications
  • Risk limits, throttles, and kill switches live in an independent control plane the model cannot modify.
  • Kill-switch operation is periodically tested at desk and firm level.
  • Trade surveillance covers AI-driven flow for manipulation patterns (spoofing-like, momentum-ignition-like behavior).
Example (CM): An execution-optimization agent is capped by risk limits enforced in a separate gateway, with a desk-level kill switch tested quarterly.
Anchors
Algorithmic trading control regimes (MiFID II RTS 6 style); market access rules (SEC 15c3-5 style); market abuse / manipulation prohibitions.
FS-AI-14

Continuous Monitoring & Drift Response

CM · WM · INS
Statement
Production AI shall be continuously monitored for performance, data and concept drift, and anomalous behavior, with defined thresholds that trigger a documented response: review, restriction, retraining, or retirement.
Rationale
Markets shift, client behavior shifts, risk regimes shift. A model validated yesterday degrades silently today; ongoing monitoring is an explicit model-risk expectation, and the response path matters as much as the alert.
Implications
  • Monitoring metrics and thresholds are defined during validation, not invented after launch.
  • Drift dashboards and alerting exist for every high-tier model.
  • A review → restrict → retire playbook is documented and rehearsed; retirement is a planned capability.
Example (WM): A churn-prediction model is auto-flagged when input distributions shift after a rate change, and is restricted pending revalidation.
Anchors
Ongoing-monitoring expectations in model risk guidance; insurance model governance bulletins; operational risk frameworks.
