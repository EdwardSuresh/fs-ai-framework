FS-AI Framework · Standout Edition

The Financial-Services Implementation Layer
for AI Governance

Fourteen principles and a reference architecture, made distinctive by four deliberate moves: field notes from practice, hardened regulatory citations, a crosswalk to NIST · ISO 42001 · EU AI Act, and a named anti-pattern gallery. Companion to FS-AI-PRIN-CAT-001 and FS-AI-RA-001.

The gap this framework occupies

Standards bodies define what to care about. Regulators define obligations. Vendors define what to buy. Almost nothing public defines how an Architecture Review Board actually gates AI in a financial institution — which evidence to demand, where controls physically live, and what the failure looks like when they don't. This framework lives in that gap: it is not another set of values; it is the implementation layer.

Not a competitor — a translator

NIST AI RMF, ISO/IEC 42001, and the EU AI Act are this framework's validation, not its rivals. The crosswalk (Move 3) maps every FS-AI principle onto all three, positioning the catalog as the financial-services-specific way to satisfy them. An institution adopting FS-AI inherits a defensible story against the major frameworks rather than a parallel one.

Field Notes — the failure each principle prevents

3 published · 14 in the full edition

Each note is an anonymized composite of failure patterns observed across the industry, written to be personalized: replace or augment these with your own ARB and program experience before publication — that substitution is precisely what makes the framework yours.

FS-AI-06
The reconstruction that failed

An exam requested reconstruction of twelve recommendations from fourteen months prior. The model had been upgraded twice and nobody pinned versions; the firm could show outputs but not reasons. Lesson: lineage is cheap daily and impossible retroactively.

FS-AI-07
The beautiful score

A third-party "lifestyle score" was the best-performing underwriting feature — because it tracked geographic patterns that mapped to protected class. Lesson: proxies hide inside the best features; vendor data must be decomposable or it must be out.

FS-AI-11
The upgrade nobody ordered

A vendor's silent model upgrade changed the tone and risk language of thousands of advisor drafts overnight; it took nine days to notice. Lesson: version pinning is a control, not a preference — and vendor contracts must make it possible.

Anti-Pattern Gallery

Nine named failures · the memorable layer

Frameworks are remembered by their opinions. Each anti-pattern has a name, a shape, an early-warning phrase you'll hear in meetings, and the principle that kills it.

Anti-Pattern 01
Demo-to-Production Drift

A pilot quietly becomes production. The gates never fire because the system never officially "launched."

⚠ You'll hear: "We'll formalize it next quarter."
Killed by FS-AI-08 — gates precede capability, and pilots have expiry dates.
Anti-Pattern 02
The Unowned Model

A model survives two reorgs with no living owner. It keeps making decisions; nobody can answer for them.

⚠ You'll hear: "I think that was Priya's team's model?"
Killed by FS-AI-01 — ownership attestation; orphaned models are restricted on sight.
Anti-Pattern 03
The Silent Vendor Update

An upstream foundation-model upgrade changes the behavior of every dependent workflow overnight, unannounced.

⚠ You'll hear: "The vendor handles all that."
Killed by FS-AI-11 — pinned versions, update notice clauses, regression suites.
Anti-Pattern 04
The Helpful Hallucination

Fluent, confident, wrong — and client-facing. The bot invents coverage, performance, or tax treatment.

⚠ You'll hear: "It's usually right."
Killed by FS-AI-10 — grounded sources, citations, hallucination testing in validation.
Anti-Pattern 05
The Proxy in the Data

The best-performing feature is a third-party score nobody can decompose — and it's tracking protected class.

⚠ You'll hear: "We don't even use demographic data."
Killed by FS-AI-07 — proxy review of all inputs, vendor data included.
Anti-Pattern 06
The Shadow Prompt

Client data flows into public chatbots through individual productivity habits, one helpful paste at a time.

⚠ You'll hear: "I just use it for formatting."
Killed by FS-AI-09 — approved-service catalog plus DLP on AI endpoints.
Anti-Pattern 07
The Black-Box Denial

An adverse decision with no articulable reason. The adverse-action letter says everything and nothing.

⚠ You'll hear: "The model is too complex to explain."
Killed by FS-AI-03 — reason codes by design, or the model isn't used for that decision.
Anti-Pattern 08
The Margin-Maximizing Recommender

"Best interest" implemented as a filter on top of an engine that optimizes the firm's revenue underneath.

⚠ You'll hear: "Compliance reviews the output anyway."
Killed by FS-AI-04 — the constraint lives inside the objective function.
Anti-Pattern 09
The Hard Stop

No degraded mode. When the AI stops, the workflow either halts completely or silently drops work on the floor.

⚠ You'll hear: "Fallback? We page IT."
Killed by FS-AI-12 — designed degraded modes, drilled like fire alarms.
"Checklists get filed. Named failures get remembered — and repeated back to you in meetings, which is how a framework spreads."
